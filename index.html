PART I
THIS WORLD

Chapter 1: What Flatland Is Like

I call our world Flatland, not because that's what we call it, but to help you understand what it's like. You, my lucky readers, live in a 3D world with depth. To understand Flatland, imagine a huge piece of paper. On this paper, imagine shapes like lines, triangles, squares, pentagons, and hexagons. These shapes aren't stuck in one place - they can move around freely on the paper's surface. But here's the catch: they can't move up or down off the paper. They're a bit like shadows, but with hard, glowing edges. That should give you a good idea of what my country and its inhabitants are like.

A few years ago, I would have said "my universe" instead of "my country." But now my mind has been opened to bigger ideas.

In a world like this, you'll quickly realize that we can't have anything truly "solid" like you do. You might think we could at least see the different shapes moving around as I've described. But no, we can't even see that much. All we can see are straight lines. Nothing else is visible to us. I know this sounds strange, but let me explain why.

Try this: Put a coin on the middle of a table. Now, look down at it from above. You'll see a circle, right? But now, start to lower your eye level towards the edge of the table. As you do this, you'll notice the coin starts to look more oval-shaped. Keep going until your eye is right at the edge of the table (this is basically how we see in Flatland). At this point, the coin won't look oval anymore – it'll just look like a straight line.

The same thing would happen with any shape cut out of cardboard – a triangle, a square, anything. When you look at it with your eye right at the table's edge, it stops looking like a shape and just appears to be a straight line.

Let's take a triangle as an example – in our world, this represents a tradesman of respectable class. Figure 1 shows how you'd see the tradesman if you were looking down from above. Figures 2 and 3 show what you'd see if your eye was getting closer to the level of the table. And if your eye was exactly level with the table (which is how we see in Flatland), you'd just see a straight line.

I've heard that your sailors have similar experiences when they're at sea and spot a distant island or coast on the horizon. The faraway land might have bays, headlands, and all sorts of angles, but from a distance, you can't see any of that. All you see is a grey, unbroken line on the water.

Well, that's exactly what we see when one of our triangular or other-shaped acquaintances comes towards us in Flatland. As they get closer, the line we see gets bigger. As they move away, it gets smaller. But it always looks like a straight line, no matter if it's actually a triangle, square, pentagon, hexagon, or circle – to us, it's just a straight line.

You might be wondering how we can tell each other apart under these circumstances. But don't worry – I'll explain that when I describe the inhabitants of Flatland. For now, let me move on to talk about our climate and houses.

Chapter 2: The Weather and Homes in Flatland

Just like in your world, we have four main directions: North, South, East, and West.

Since we don't have a sun or any other celestial bodies, we can't figure out which way is North in the usual way. But we have our own method. In Flatland, there's a natural, constant pull towards the South. In milder climates, this pull is pretty weak – even a woman in decent health can travel several miles northward without much trouble. But this southward pull, though subtle, is enough to act as a compass in most parts of our world.

Also, it helps that the rain (which falls at regular times) always comes from the North. In towns, we can use the houses as guides too. Their side walls usually run North-South, so the roofs can keep off the rain from the North. In the countryside where there aren't any houses, we use tree trunks as a kind of guide. All in all, we don't have as much trouble figuring out our directions as you might think.

But in our more temperate regions, where you can barely feel the pull southward, it can be tricky. I've sometimes found myself stuck for hours in a completely empty plain with no houses or trees to guide me, waiting for the rain to start so I could continue my journey. This southward pull is much harder on the weak, elderly, and especially on delicate women. That's why it's considered polite, when you meet a woman on the street, to always give her the North side of the path. This isn't always easy to do quickly when you're in good health and in a climate where it's hard to tell North from South.

Our houses don't have windows. Light comes to us the same way whether we're inside or outside, day or night, all the time and everywhere. We don't know where it comes from. In the old days, our scholars used to wonder a lot about "Where does light come from?" Trying to answer this question just led to a lot of people ending up in mental asylums. So, after failed attempts to discourage this research indirectly by taxing it heavily, the government eventually just banned it outright.

I – sadly, I alone in Flatland – now know the true answer to this mysterious question. But I can't make any of my countrymen understand my knowledge. They mock me – the only one who knows the truths of Space and the theory of light coming from the world of three dimensions. They treat me like I'm the craziest of the crazy! But let's not dwell on these painful thoughts. Let's get back to talking about our homes.

The most common shape for a house is five-sided or pentagonal, as shown in the diagram. The two northern sides, RO and OF, make up the roof, and usually don't have any doors. There's a small door on the East side for women, and a much larger one on the West side for men. The South side, or floor, typically doesn't have a door.

Square and triangular houses aren't allowed. Here's why: The corners of a square (and even more so of an equilateral triangle) are much sharper than those of a pentagon. Also, the outlines of non-living things (like houses) are harder to see than the outlines of men and women. So there's a real risk that the sharp corners of a square or triangular house could seriously hurt a careless or distracted traveler who might run into them. That's why, as far back as the 11th century of our era, triangular houses were completely banned by law. The only exceptions are fortifications, gunpowder storage, army barracks, and other government buildings that the general public shouldn't approach without being careful.

At that time, square houses were still allowed everywhere, though they were discouraged with a special tax. But about three centuries later, the law decided that in all towns with more than 10,000 people, the smallest angle allowed for a house, for public safety reasons, would be that of a pentagon. The good sense of the community has supported the government's efforts. Now, even in the countryside, almost all houses are pentagonal. It's only occasionally, in some really remote and backward farming areas, that you might still find a square house.

Chapter 3: The People of Flatland

The biggest fully-grown inhabitant of Flatland might measure about eleven of your inches from end to end. Twelve inches would be considered extremely tall.

Our women are straight lines.

Our soldiers and lowest-class workers are triangles with two equal sides. Each of these sides is about eleven inches long, with a base (the third side) so short (often not even half an inch) that they form a very sharp and dangerous angle at their point. In fact, when their bases are extremely short (not more than an eighth of an inch), you can barely tell them apart from straight lines or women - their points are that sharp. Like in your world, we call these triangles "isosceles," and that's how I'll refer to them from now on.

Our middle class consists of equilateral triangles - triangles with all sides equal.

Our professionals and gentlemen are squares (which is what I am) and pentagons (five-sided figures).

Above these are the nobility, with several ranks starting from hexagons (six-sided figures) and increasing in the number of sides until they earn the respected title of "polygonal" or many-sided. Finally, when a shape has so many sides that you can't distinguish it from a circle, it's considered part of the circular or priestly order - the highest class of all.

In our society, it's a law of nature that a male child will have one more side than his father. So, each generation usually moves up one step in social status and nobility. For example, the son of a square is a pentagon, the son of a pentagon is a hexagon, and so on.

But this rule doesn't always apply to tradesmen, and even less to soldiers and workers. These lower classes can hardly be called human figures at all, since their sides aren't all equal. For them, nature's law doesn't hold, and the son of an isosceles (a triangle with two equal sides) usually remains an isosceles.

However, there's still hope for the isosceles class. After a long string of military victories or hard, skilled labor, the smarter ones among the artisan and soldier classes might develop a slightly longer third side (or base) and their other two sides might shrink a bit. Marriages (arranged by priests) between the sons and daughters of these more intelligent lower-class members often result in children that are closer to being equilateral triangles.

It's rare - compared to the huge number of isosceles births - for a true, certified equilateral triangle to be born to isosceles parents. This kind of birth requires not only a series of carefully planned marriages but also long-term self-control and moderation by the would-be ancestors, and a patient, systematic development of the isosceles intellect over many generations.

When a true equilateral triangle is born to isosceles parents, it's cause for celebration for miles around. After a thorough examination by the Health and Social Services Board, if the infant is certified as regular, it's officially admitted into the equilateral class in a solemn ceremony. The baby is then taken from its proud but sad parents and adopted by a childless equilateral couple. The adoptive parents must swear never to let the child enter its former home or even look at its birth family again. This is to prevent the newly developed shape from unconsciously imitating its family and sliding back to its hereditary level.

The occasional emergence of an equilateral from lower-class ancestors is welcomed not only by the poor lower classes themselves, as a glimmer of hope in their monotonous lives, but also by the upper classes. The higher classes know that while these rare cases don't really threaten their own privileges, they serve as a useful barrier against revolution from below.

If the sharp-angled lower classes had no hope or ambition at all, they might have found leaders in their many rebellions who were smart enough to make their superior numbers and strength too much even for the wisdom of the circles to handle. But nature has wisely decreed that as the working classes become more intelligent and virtuous, their sharp angles (which make them physically dangerous) also increase and become more like the relatively harmless angle of an equilateral triangle. Even in the most brutal and formidable soldier class - creatures almost as unintelligent as women - we find that as they develop the mental ability to use their tremendous piercing power effectively, they lose some of that piercing power itself.

How wonderful is this law of compensation! It perfectly proves the natural fitness and, I might even say, the divine origin of the aristocratic constitution of Flatland! By cleverly using this law of nature, the polygons and circles can almost always stop rebellion in its earliest stages. They take advantage of the irrepressible hopefulness of the human mind. Through art and science, they can often make some of the more intelligent rebel leaders perfectly regular, admitting them immediately into the privileged classes. A much larger number, who are still below standard, are tempted by the prospect of eventually being ennobled and are persuaded to enter state hospitals, where they're kept in comfortable confinement for life. Only one or two of the most stubborn, foolish, and hopelessly irregular are executed.

Then the miserable masses of isosceles, without plans or leaders, are either quickly defeated by the small group of their brothers that the Chief Circle keeps on standby for emergencies, or more often, they're manipulated by the circular party into fighting among themselves, destroying each other with their own angles. Our history records no less than 120 rebellions, not counting smaller uprisings numbered at 235, and they've all ended this way.

Chapter 4: About the Women

If you think our very pointy triangles of the soldier class are dangerous, you can imagine how much more dangerous our women are. If a soldier is like a wedge, a woman is like a needle - she's all point, at least at her two ends. Add to this the fact that she can make herself practically invisible at will, and you'll understand why a female in Flatland is not someone to be taken lightly.

But here, some of my younger readers might ask, "How can a woman in Flatland make herself invisible?" This should be obvious without explanation. But let me clarify it for you.

Place a needle on a table. Now, look at it from the side, with your eye level with the table. You can see its whole length, right? But look at it end-on, and you see nothing but a point - it's become practically invisible. It's the same with our women. When her side is facing us, we see her as a straight line. When the end containing her eye or mouth (for us, these two organs are the same) is facing us, we see nothing but a very bright point. But when her back is towards us, since it's not as shiny and is almost as dim as an inanimate object, it acts like an Invisible Cap.

Now you can see the dangers we face from our women. If even the angle of a respectable middle-class triangle isn't without its dangers, if bumping into a working man can cause a gash, if a collision with a military officer can result in a serious wound, if just a touch from the point of a common soldier can be fatal - what do you think would happen if you ran into a woman? It would be nothing short of instant destruction. And when a woman is invisible, or visible only as a dim, faint point, how hard must it be, even for the most careful, to always avoid colliding with her?

Many laws have been made at different times in different states of Flatland to minimize this danger. In the southern, hotter climates, where gravity is stronger and people are more likely to move around accidentally, the laws about women are naturally much stricter. But you can get a general idea of the rules from this summary:

1. Every house must have one entrance on the east side, for women only. All females must enter "in a proper and respectful manner" through this door, not through the men's or western door.

2. No female can walk in any public place without constantly making a peace-cry, under penalty of death.

3. Any female certified to be suffering from St. Vitus's Dance, fits, chronic cold with violent sneezing, or any disease causing involuntary movements, must be immediately destroyed.

In some states, there's an additional law that forbids females, under penalty of death, from standing or walking in any public place without moving their backs constantly from right to left to show their presence to those behind them. Other states require a woman, when traveling, to be followed by one of her sons, servants, or her husband. Some states even confine women to their houses except during religious festivals. But the wisest of our leaders have found that increasing restrictions on females not only weakens and reduces the population but also leads to more domestic murders, to the point where a state loses more than it gains from too many rules.

Whenever women's tempers are aggravated by being confined at home or by too many rules when they're out, they tend to take out their anger on their husbands and children. In the hotter climates, the entire male population of a village has sometimes been wiped out in just an hour or two during a simultaneous female outburst. So, the three laws mentioned above are enough for the better-governed states and can be seen as a rough example of our Female Code.

After all, our main protection comes not from laws but from the interests of the women themselves. Although they can cause instant death with a backward movement, if they can't quickly disengage their stinging end from their victim's struggling body, their own fragile bodies are likely to be shattered.

Fashion is also on our side. I mentioned earlier that in some less civilized states, no female is allowed to stand in any public place without swaying her back from right to left. This practice has been universal among well-bred ladies in all well-governed states for as long as anyone can remember. It's considered a disgrace to any state if they have to enforce by law what should be, and is in every respectable female, a natural instinct. The rhythmic and, if I may say so, well-modulated swaying of the back in our circular-rank ladies is envied and imitated by the wife of a common equilateral, who can only manage a monotonous swing, like the ticking of a pendulum. And the regular tick of the equilateral is just as admired and copied by the wife of the progressive and ambitious isosceles, in whose family no "back-motion" of any kind has yet become necessary for life. So, in every family of status and importance, "back-motion" is as common as time itself, and the husbands and sons in these households are at least safe from invisible attacks.

Not that you should think for a moment that our women are without affection. But unfortunately, in the weaker sex, the passion of the moment tends to overrule every other consideration. This is, of course, a necessity arising from their unfortunate shape. Since they have no angles at all, being even lower in this respect than the lowest isosceles, they are completely devoid of brainpower and have no ability to think, judge, or plan ahead, and hardly any memory. So in their fits of fury, they forget all claims and recognize no distinctions. I've actually known a case where a woman wiped out her entire household, and half an hour later, when her rage was over and the remains had been cleared away, she asked what had happened to her husband and children.

Obviously then, a woman should not be irritated as long as she's in a position where she can turn around. When you have them in their apartments - which are designed to prevent them from turning - you can say and do what you like. They're then completely harmless, and a few minutes later, they won't remember the incident for which they might have been threatening you with death just moments ago, nor the promises you might have had to make to calm them down.

On the whole, we get along pretty smoothly in our domestic relationships, except in the lower levels of the military classes. There, the lack of tact and discretion on the part of the husbands sometimes leads to indescribable disasters. Relying too much on the offensive weapons of their sharp angles instead of using good sense and timely pretense, these reckless creatures often neglect to build their wives' apartments properly, or they irritate their wives with ill-advised words outside and then refuse to take them back. Moreover, a blunt and stubborn regard for literal truth makes them unwilling to make the lavish promises that a more sensible circle can use to instantly pacify his wife. The result is massacre, though not without its advantages, as it eliminates the more brutal and troublesome of the isosceles. Many of our circles see the destructiveness of the thinner sex as one of many providential arrangements for controlling excess population and nipping revolution in the bud.

Yet even in our best-regulated and most nearly circular families, I can't say that the ideal of family life is as high as it is in your Spaceland. There is peace, in the sense that there's no slaughter, but there's necessarily little harmony in tastes or pursuits. The cautious wisdom of the circles has ensured safety at the cost of domestic comfort. In every circular or polygonal household, it has been a habit from time immemorial - and now has become a kind of instinct among the women of our higher classes - that the mothers and daughters should constantly keep their eyes and mouths towards their husband and his male friends. For a lady in a distinguished family to turn her back on her husband would be seen as a kind of omen, involving loss of status. But, as I'll soon show, this custom, though it has the advantage of safety, is not without its downsides.

In the house of the working man or respectable tradesman - where the wife is allowed to turn her back on her husband while doing her household chores - there are at least moments of quiet, when the wife is neither seen nor heard, except for the humming sound of the continuous peace-cry. But in the homes of the upper classes, there is often no peace at all. There, the chattering mouth and bright, piercing eye are always directed towards the master of the household, and light itself is not more persistent than the stream of female conversation. The tact and skill that are enough to avoid a woman's sting are not enough to stop a woman's mouth. And since the wife has absolutely nothing to say, and absolutely no constraint of wit, sense, or conscience to prevent her from saying it, quite a few cynics have said that they prefer the danger of the deadly but silent sting to the safe but constant chatter from a woman's other end.

To my Spaceland readers, the condition of our women may seem truly pitiful, and so indeed it is. A male of the lowest type of isosceles may hope for some improvement of his angle and the eventual elevation of his whole degraded class, but no woman can entertain such hopes for her sex. "Once a woman, always a woman" is a decree of nature, and even the laws of evolution seem suspended in her disfavor. Yet at least we can admire the wise arrangement which has ordained that, as they have no hopes, so they shall have no memory to recall, and no foresight to anticipate, the miseries and humiliations which are at once a necessity of their existence and the basis of the constitution of Flatland.

Chapter 5: How We Recognize Each Other

You, who are blessed with both shade and light, who have two eyes and understand perspective, who can enjoy various colors, who can actually see an angle and view the complete circumference of a circle in your happy three-dimensional world - how can I make you understand the extreme difficulty we face in Flatland when it comes to recognizing each other?

Remember what I told you earlier: All beings in Flatland, living or not, no matter what their shape, appear to us as straight lines. How then can we tell one figure from another, when they all look the same?

The answer is threefold. The first method of recognition is hearing, which for us is much more developed than it is for you. It allows us not only to distinguish our personal friends by their voices but even to tell different classes apart, at least for the three lowest orders: the equilateral, the square, and the pentagon. (I'm not counting the isosceles here.) But as we move up the social ladder, the process of distinguishing by voice becomes more difficult, partly because voices become more similar, and partly because the ability to distinguish voices is considered a common skill, not much valued by the aristocracy. And whenever there's any risk of imposture, we can't rely on this method. Among our lowest classes, the vocal organs are developed to a degree that's more than matched by their hearing ability, so an isosceles can easily imitate the voice of a polygon, and with some training, even that of a circle. So a second method is more commonly used.

Feeling is the main way of recognition among our women and lower classes - I'll talk about our upper classes later - especially between strangers, and when the question is about class rather than individual identity. What "introduction" means among the higher classes in Spaceland, the process of "feeling" is for us. "Allow me to ask you to feel and be felt by my friend Mr. So-and-so" is still the usual way of introduction among the more old-fashioned country gentlemen in remote areas. But in towns and among businessmen, the words "be felt by" are left out, and the sentence is shortened to, "Let me ask you to feel Mr. So-and-so," although it's assumed, of course, that the "feeling" will be mutual. Among our more modern and dashing young gentlemen - who are extremely averse to unnecessary effort and supremely indifferent to the purity of our language - the phrase is further shortened by using "to feel" in a technical sense, meaning "to recommend for the purposes of feeling and being felt." At this moment, the slang of polite or fast society in the upper classes allows such a barbarism as "Mr. Smith, let me feel Mr. Jones."

Don't think, however, that "feeling" is as tedious a process for us as it would be for you, or that we need to feel all around every individual before we can determine their class. Long practice and training, starting in schools and continuing through daily life, enable us to distinguish immediately by touch between the angles of an equilateral triangle, a square, and a pentagon. And I hardly need to say that the brainless point of a sharp-angled isosceles is obvious even to the dullest touch. So it's usually not necessary to do more than feel a single angle of an individual; once this is done, we know the class of the person we're addressing, unless they belong to the higher sections of the nobility. There, the difficulty is much greater. Even a Master of Arts from our University of Wentbridge has been known to confuse a ten-sided with a twelve-sided polygon, and there's hardly a Doctor of Science in or out of that famous university who could quickly and confidently distinguish between a twenty-sided and a twenty-four-sided member of the aristocracy.

Those of my readers who recall the extracts I gave earlier from the legislative code concerning women will easily understand that the process of introduction by contact requires some care and discretion. Otherwise, the angles might inflict irreparable injury on the unwary person doing the feeling. It's essential for the safety of the feeler that the person being felt should stand perfectly still. A sudden start, a fidgety change of position, or even a violent sneeze has been known to prove fatal to the careless and to nip many a promising friendship in the bud. This is especially true among the lower classes of triangles. With them, the eye is located so far from their vertex that they can hardly notice what's happening at that end of their body. Moreover, they're of a rough, coarse nature, not sensitive to the delicate touch of the highly organized polygon. So it's no wonder that an involuntary toss of the head has sometimes deprived the state of a valuable life!

I've heard that my excellent grandfather - one of the least irregular of his unhappy isosceles class, who indeed obtained, shortly before his death, four out of seven votes from the Sanitary and Social Board for passing him into the equal-sided class - often spoke tearfully of an accident of this kind that happened to his great-great-great-grandfather, a respectable working man with a brain angle of 59°30'. According to his story, my unfortunate ancestor, suffering from rheumatism and in the act of being felt by a polygon, accidentally stabbed the great man with a sudden start, partly through his diagonal. This incident, combined with his long imprisonment and degradation, and partly because of the moral shock that affected all of my ancestor's relations, set our family back a degree and a half in their climb towards better things. As a result, in the next generation, the family brain was registered at only 58°, and it took five generations to recover the lost ground, reach the full 60°, and finally achieve the rise from the isosceles class.

At this point, I think I hear some of my better-educated readers exclaim, "How could you in Flatland know anything about angles and degrees or minutes? We can see an angle because we can see two straight lines meeting, but you, who can only see one straight line at a time, or at most a number of bits of straight lines all in one straight line - how can you ever recognize an angle, let alone measure angles of different sizes?"

I answer that though we can't see angles, we can infer them, and with great precision. Our sense of touch, sharpened by necessity and developed through long training, allows us to distinguish angles far more accurately than your sense of sight can, without the help of a ruler or protractor. Nor should I forget to explain that we have great natural aids. It's a law of nature for us that the brain of the isosceles class begins at half a degree, or thirty minutes, and increases (if it increases at all) by half a degree in each generation until the goal of 60° is reached. At this point, the condition of serfdom is left behind, and the freeman enters the class of regulars.

Consequently, nature itself provides us with an ascending scale or alphabet of angles from half a degree up to 60°, examples of which are placed in every elementary school throughout the land. Due to occasional setbacks, even more frequent moral and intellectual stagnation, and the extraordinary fertility of the criminal and vagrant classes, there's always a vast surplus of individuals of the half-degree and single-degree class, and a fair abundance of specimens up to 10°. These are completely lacking in civil rights, and a great number of them, not even having enough intelligence for warfare, are devoted by the states to the service of education. Chained immovably to remove all possibility of danger, they're placed in the classrooms of our infant schools, where they're used by the Board of Education to teach tact and intelligence to the offspring of the middle classes - qualities that these wretched creatures themselves completely lack.

In some states, the specimens are occasionally fed and allowed to live for several years. But in the more temperate and better-regulated regions, it's found in the long run to be more advantageous for the educational interests of the young to do without food and to renew the specimens every month - which is about the average lifespan of the foodless criminal class. In the cheaper schools, what's gained by the longer existence of the specimen is lost, partly in the cost of food, and partly in the reduced accuracy of the angles, which deteriorate after a few weeks of constant "feeling." We shouldn't forget to add, when listing the advantages of the more expensive system, that it tends, though slightly but noticeably, to reduce the surplus isosceles population - a goal that every statesman in Flatland constantly keeps in mind. On the whole, therefore - although I'm not unaware that in many popularly elected school boards, there's a reaction in favor of what's called "the cheap system" - I'm inclined to think that this is one of the many cases where expense is the truest economy.

But I mustn't allow questions of school board politics to distract me from my subject. Enough has been said, I trust, to show that recognition by feeling is not as tedious or uncertain a process as might have been thought, and it's obviously more reliable than recognition by hearing. Still, as has been pointed out above, the objection remains that this method is not without danger. For this reason, many in the middle and lower classes, and all without exception in the polygonal and circular orders, prefer a third method, which I'll describe in the next section.

Chapter 6: How We Recognize Each Other by Sight

I know I might sound inconsistent here. In earlier sections, I said that all figures in Flatland look like straight lines, and I implied that it's impossible to visually tell different classes apart. But now I'm going to explain how we can recognize each other by sight.

If you look back, though, you'll see that I said this kind of recognition is only "universal among the lower classes." It's only in the higher classes and in our milder climates that we use sight recognition.

The reason we can do this at all is because of fog. In your world, fog is just an annoyance that blocks the view and makes people gloomy. But for us, it's almost as important as air. We see it as the source of arts and the parent of sciences. Let me explain what I mean.

If there was no fog, all lines would look equally clear, no matter how far away they were. This is actually the case in those unlucky countries where the air is perfectly dry and clear. But where there's a good amount of fog, objects that are further away look dimmer than those that are closer. Even a difference of a few inches can be noticeable. By carefully observing these differences in brightness, we can figure out the shape of what we're looking at.

Let me give you an example to make this clearer.

Suppose I see two individuals approaching and I want to figure out their rank. Let's say they're a Merchant and a Physician - in other words, an Equilateral Triangle and a Pentagon. How can I tell them apart?

Any child in Spaceland who's studied a bit of geometry would understand that if I can position my eye so that it bisects an angle of the approaching stranger, my view will be evenly between the two sides closest to me. This way, both sides will appear the same size to me.

Now, in the case of the Merchant (the Triangle), what will I see? I'll see a straight line DAE, where the middle point (A) will be very bright because it's closest to me. But on either side, the line will quickly fade into dimness, because the sides AC and AB quickly recede into the fog. The Merchant's extremities, D and E, will be very dim indeed.

On the other hand, with the Physician (the Pentagon), I'll also see a line (D'A'E') with a bright center (A'). But it will fade away less quickly, because its sides (A'C', A'B') don't recede into the fog as rapidly. The Physician's extremities, D' and E', won't be as dim as the Merchant's.

From these two examples, you can probably understand how - after a lot of training and constant practice - the well-educated classes among us can fairly accurately tell the difference between the middle and lower orders by sight. If my Spaceland readers can grasp this general idea and see how it might be possible, I've achieved all I can reasonably expect.

If I tried to give more details, I'd probably just confuse you. But for the sake of young and inexperienced readers, who might think from my simple examples of recognizing my Father and Sons that sight recognition is easy, I should point out that in real life, most recognition problems are much more subtle and complex.

For example, if my Father (a Triangle) approaches me sideways instead of point-first, I might not be sure at first if he's a Triangle or a straight line (in other words, a Woman). I'd have to ask him to rotate or I'd have to move my eye around him to be sure.

Or if I'm with one of my hexagonal Grandsons and I'm looking straight at one of his sides, I'll see one whole line (AB) that's quite bright (barely fading at the ends) and two smaller lines (CA and BD) that are dim throughout and fade away towards the ends C and D.

But I shouldn't go into too much detail on this. Even the least skilled mathematician in Spaceland will believe me when I say that the problems of life become very complex for well-educated Flatlanders when they're in motion - rotating, advancing, or retreating - and trying to distinguish between several high-ranking Polygons moving in different directions. Imagine trying to do this at a ball or a party! These challenges fully justify the extensive education of our Learned Professors of Geometry, both Static and Kinetic, at the great University of Wentbridge, where Sight Recognition is taught as both a science and an art to large classes of the elite of the States.

Only a few of the sons from our noblest and wealthiest houses can afford the time and money needed to fully master this great and valuable Art. Even for me, a Mathematician of some standing and the Grandfather of two promising young Hexagons, it can be very confusing to find myself in the middle of a crowd of rotating Polygons of the higher classes. And of course, for a common Tradesman or Serf, such a sight would be almost as incomprehensible as it would be for you if you were suddenly transported to my country.

In such a crowd, all you would see around you is a Line, seemingly straight, but with parts that constantly change in brightness. Even if you had completed your third year in the Pentagonal and Hexagonal classes at the University, and were perfect in the theory, you'd still need many years of experience before you could move in a fashionable crowd without bumping into your betters. It's against etiquette to ask them to "feel" you, and they, with their superior culture and breeding, know all about your movements, while you know very little or nothing about theirs. In short, to behave perfectly in Polygonal society, you really need to be a Polygon yourself. At least, that's what my painful experience has taught me.

It's amazing how much the Art - or I might almost call it instinct - of Sight Recognition develops with regular practice and by avoiding the habit of "Feeling." It's like how, in your world, deaf and mute people who are allowed to use gestures and hand-alphabet never really learn the more difficult but far more valuable arts of lip-speech and lip-reading. It's the same with us when it comes to "Seeing" and "Feeling." Anyone who relies on "Feeling" in early life will never truly master "Seeing."

For this reason, among our Higher Classes, "Feeling" is discouraged or completely forbidden. From the cradle, their children are sent to higher, exclusive schools instead of the public elementary schools where the art of Feeling is taught. At our prestigious University, "feeling" is seen as a serious offense, resulting in expulsion for a second incident.

But among the lower classes, the art of Sight Recognition is seen as an unattainable luxury. A common Tradesman can't afford to let his son spend a third of his life in abstract studies. So the children of the poor are allowed to "feel" from their earliest years. This gives them an early cleverness and liveliness that at first seems to compare favorably with the inactive, undeveloped, and listless behavior of the half-educated youths of the Polygonal class. But when these Polygonal youths finally complete their University course and are ready to put their theory into practice, the change in them is almost like a new birth. In every art, science, and social pursuit, they quickly catch up to and surpass their Triangular competitors.

Only a small minority of the Polygonal Class fail to pass the Final Test or Leaving Examination at the University. The condition of this unsuccessful minority is truly pitiful. Rejected by the higher class, they're also looked down upon by the lower. They don't have the mature and systematically trained powers of the Polygonal Bachelors and Masters of Arts, nor do they have the native quick-wittedness and versatility of the young Tradesmen. The professions and public services are closed to them, and although in most States they're not actually barred from marriage, they have the greatest difficulty in forming suitable alliances. Their offspring is generally unfortunate, if not downright Irregular.

It's from these unsuccessful members of our Nobility that the great Tumults and Seditions of past ages have usually gotten their leaders. The damage caused by this has been so great that an increasing minority of our more progressive Statesmen believe that true mercy would require completely suppressing them. They argue that all those who fail to pass the Final Examination of the University should either be imprisoned for life or humanely put to death.

But I find I've wandered off topic into the subject of Irregularities, a matter of such vital interest that it deserves its own separate section.

Chapter 7: About Irregular Shapes

Throughout the previous pages, I've been assuming something that perhaps should have been clearly stated at the beginning as a fundamental principle: every human being in Flatland is a Regular Figure. By this, I mean that a Woman must not only be a line, but a perfectly straight line; an Artisan or Soldier must have two equal sides; Tradesmen must have three equal sides; Lawyers (like myself) must have four equal sides, and generally, all Polygons must have all their sides equal.

The size of the sides would, of course, depend on the individual's age. A newborn Female would be about an inch long, while a tall adult Woman might be up to a foot long. As for Males of every class, you could roughly say that the total length of an adult's sides is two feet or a little more. But we're not concerned about the size of our sides here. I'm talking about the equality of sides, and it doesn't take much thought to see that our entire social life in Flatland is based on the fundamental fact that Nature intends all Figures to have equal sides.

If our sides were unequal, our angles might be unequal too. Instead of being able to determine someone's shape by feeling or seeing just one angle, we'd have to check each angle by Feeling. But life would be too short for such tedious groping. The whole science and art of Sight Recognition would immediately die out; Feeling, as an art, wouldn't survive much longer; social interactions would become dangerous or impossible; there would be no more confidence or foresight; in short, civilization might relapse into barbarism.

Am I going too fast for my Readers to follow these obvious conclusions? Surely a moment's reflection and a single example from everyday life must convince everyone that our social system is based on Regularity, or Equality of Angles. For instance, you might meet two or three Tradesmen in the street, whom you immediately recognize as Tradesmen by glancing at their angles and quickly fading sides. You invite them into your house for lunch. You do this confidently because everyone knows to within an inch or two the area occupied by an adult Triangle. But imagine if your Tradesman dragged behind his respectable and regular vertex a parallelogram twelve or thirteen inches long - what would you do with such a monster stuck in your house door?

But I'm insulting the intelligence of my Readers by piling up details that must be obvious to anyone who enjoys living in Spaceland. Clearly, measuring a single angle would no longer be enough in such bizarre circumstances; you'd spend your whole life feeling or surveying the perimeter of your acquaintances. The difficulties of avoiding collisions in a crowd are already enough to tax the wisdom of even a well-educated Square; but if no one could calculate the Regularity of a single figure in the group, everything would be chaos and confusion. The slightest panic could cause serious injuries, or if there happened to be any Women or Soldiers present, perhaps considerable loss of life.

Therefore, both practical necessity and Nature approve of Regularity in shape. "Irregularity of Figure" means the same to us as, or even more than, a combination of moral wrongness and criminality does to you, and we treat it accordingly. There are some people who argue that there's no necessary connection between geometric and moral Irregularity. They say, "The Irregular are rejected by their own parents from birth, mocked by their siblings, ignored by servants, scorned and suspected by society, and excluded from all positions of responsibility, trust, and useful activity. Their every movement is closely watched by the police until they come of age and present themselves for inspection. Then they're either destroyed if they're found to exceed the allowed margin of deviation, or given an uninteresting job for a miserable wage. They're forced to live and eat at the office, and even take their vacation under close supervision. Is it any wonder that human nature, even in the best and purest, becomes bitter and twisted in such an environment?"

All this very plausible reasoning doesn't convince me, just as it hasn't convinced the wisest of our Statesmen, that our ancestors were wrong in establishing the principle that tolerating Irregularity is incompatible with the safety of the State. Undoubtedly, the life of an Irregular is hard; but the interests of the Many require that it should be hard. If a man with a triangular front and a polygonal back were allowed to exist and have children who were even more Irregular, what would become of the arts of life? Would the houses and doors and churches in Flatland have to be altered to accommodate such monsters? Would our ticket collectors have to measure every man's perimeter before allowing him into a theater or lecture room? Should an Irregular be exempted from military service? And if not, how could we prevent him from causing havoc in the ranks of his comrades? Again, what irresistible temptations to fraudulent impostures such a creature would face! How easy it would be for him to enter a shop with his polygonal front first, and order goods to any extent from a trusting tradesman! Let the advocates of a falsely called Philanthropy plead as they may for the abolition of the Irregular Penal Laws, I for one have never known an Irregular who wasn't also what Nature clearly intended him to be - a hypocrite, a misanthrope, and, to the extent of his power, a troublemaker.

Not that I would recommend (at present) the extreme measures adopted by some States, where an infant whose angle deviates by half a degree from the correct angle is immediately destroyed at birth. Some of our highest and most capable men, men of real genius, have during their earliest days suffered from deviations as great as, or even greater than, forty-five minutes: and the loss of their valuable lives would have been an irreparable injury to the State. The art of healing has also achieved some of its most glorious triumphs in the compressions, extensions, trepannings, colligations, and other surgical or dietary operations by which Irregularity has been partly or wholly cured. Advocating therefore a middle path, I would not lay down any fixed or absolute line of demarcation; but at the period when the frame is just beginning to set, and when the Medical Board has reported that recovery is improbable, I would suggest that the Irregular offspring be painlessly and mercifully put to death.

Chapter 8: The Old Custom of Painting

If you've been following me closely up to this point, you won't be surprised to hear that life in Flatland can be pretty dull. Now, I don't mean that we don't have battles, conspiracies, uprisings, factions, and all those other things that are supposed to make history interesting. And I'm not saying that the strange mix of life's problems and math problems, which constantly makes us guess and lets us check our guesses right away, doesn't add a zest to our existence that you in Spaceland might not understand. What I'm talking about now is the aesthetic and artistic side of life. From that point of view, our life is dull - really, really dull.

How could it be otherwise? All our scenery, all our historical paintings, all our portraits, flowers, still life - everything we see is just a single line. The only variety we get is in how bright or dark that line is.

But it wasn't always like this. If tradition tells the truth, color brought a brief period of splendor to the lives of our ancestors for about half a dozen centuries or so, a very long time ago.

The story goes that a private individual - a Pentagon, though reports vary on the exact name - accidentally discovered what makes up the simpler colors and figured out a basic way of painting. They say he started by decorating his house, then his slaves, then his father, his sons and grandsons, and finally himself. The results were both convenient and beautiful, and everyone liked them. Wherever this person (let's call him Chromatistes, which is the name most trusted sources agree on) went with his colorful body, he immediately attracted attention and respect. Nobody needed to "feel" him anymore; no one mistook his front for his back; everyone could easily tell which way he was moving without having to calculate anything; nobody bumped into him or failed to make way for him; he didn't have to strain his voice shouting to announce who he was, like we colorless Squares and Pentagons often have to do when we're in a crowd.

The fashion spread like wildfire. Within a week, every Square and Triangle in the district had copied Chromatistes' example. Only a few of the more conservative Pentagons still held out. A month or two later, even the Dodecagons had caught the bug. Before a year had passed, the habit had spread to everyone except the very highest of the Nobility.

Naturally, it wasn't long before the custom made its way from Chromatistes' district to surrounding regions. Within two generations, everyone in all of Flatland was colorful - except for the Women and the Priests.

Here, Nature herself seemed to put up a barrier and argue against extending this innovation to these two classes. The argument went like this: "Having many sides is meant to imply having many colors." This catchy phrase flew from mouth to mouth, converting whole towns to the new culture at once. But obviously, this saying didn't apply to our Priests and Women. Women only have one side (or, to be pedantic, no sides at all). As for the Priests, if they truly wanted to be seen as perfect Circles and not just very high-class Polygons with an infinite number of infinitesimally small sides, they had to boast (as Women admitted and regretted) that they too had no sides, being blessed with a perimeter of just one line - in other words, a Circumference. So it happened that these two Classes couldn't see any logic in the so-called rule about "Having many sides means having many colors." When everyone else had given in to the temptation of body decoration, only the Priests and Women remained pure and unpainted.

Call them immoral, wild, anarchic, unscientific - use whatever names you want - but from an artistic point of view, those ancient days of the Color Revolt were the glorious childhood of Art in Flatland. Sadly, it was a childhood that never grew into adulthood or even reached its teenage years. Back then, just living was a joy because living meant seeing. Even a small party was a visual treat. The richly varied colors of a crowd in a church or theater are said to have sometimes been too distracting even for our greatest teachers and actors. But the most breathtaking sight of all is said to have been the indescribable magnificence of a military review.

Imagine the sight of a battle line of twenty thousand Isosceles soldiers suddenly turning around, switching from the somber black of their bases to the orange of their two sides including their sharp angle. Picture the militia of Equilateral Triangles in red, white, and blue; the artillery-men Squares in mauve, ultramarine, gamboge, and burnt umber, rapidly spinning near their crimson guns; the dashing and flashing of the five-colored and six-colored Pentagons and Hexagons racing across the field as surgeons, geometers, and aides-de-camp. All of this was surely enough to make believable the famous story of how an illustrious Circle, overcome by the artistic beauty of the forces under his command, threw aside his marshal's baton and his royal crown, declaring that from then on, he would exchange them for an artist's brush.

The sensory richness of those days must have been incredible. We can see hints of it even in the language and vocabulary of the time. The everyday speech of ordinary citizens during the Color Revolt seems to have been infused with richer tones of word and thought. We still owe our finest poetry and whatever rhythm remains in our more scientific speech to that era.

Chapter 9: The Universal Color Bill

While all this was happening, the intellectual arts were rapidly declining.

The Art of Sight Recognition, no longer necessary, fell out of practice. Studies like Geometry, Statics, Kinetics, and other related subjects soon came to be seen as unnecessary and fell into disrespect and neglect, even at our University. The simpler Art of Feeling quickly met the same fate in our Elementary Schools.

Then the Isosceles classes (lower-class triangles) started causing trouble. They claimed that the Specimens (used for teaching Feeling) were no longer needed, and they refused to pay the usual taxes from the Criminal classes for Education. They grew more numerous and more insolent every day, emboldened by their immunity from the old burden that used to both tame their brutal nature and keep their numbers in check.

Year after year, the Soldiers and Artisans began to argue more forcefully - and with increasing truth - that there wasn't much difference between them and the very highest class of Polygons. Now that they were considered equal to the higher classes and could handle all of life's problems, whether they involved movement or not, simply by recognizing colors, they felt they were just as good. They weren't content with the natural decline of Sight Recognition. They boldly demanded that the law prohibit all "monopolizing and aristocratic Arts" and consequently abolish all funding for studies in Sight Recognition, Mathematics, and Feeling. Soon, they began to insist that since Color, which they saw as a second Nature, had eliminated the need for aristocratic distinctions, the Law should follow suit. They demanded that from now on, all individuals and all classes should be recognized as absolutely equal and entitled to equal rights.

Finding the higher Orders wavering and undecided, the leaders of the Revolution pushed their demands even further. They finally insisted that all classes, including the Priests and the Women, should honor Color by allowing themselves to be painted. When it was objected that Priests and Women had no sides, they retorted that Nature and Practicality agreed that the front half of every human being (that is, the half containing the eye and mouth) should be distinguishable from the back half. They therefore brought before a general and extraordinary Assembly of all the States of Flatland a Bill proposing that for every Woman, the half containing the eye and mouth should be colored red, and the other half green. The Priests were to be painted in the same way, with red applied to the semicircle where the eye and mouth were in the middle, and the other or back semicircle colored green.

There was a lot of cunning in this proposal. It didn't come from any Isosceles - no being that degraded would have the intelligence to appreciate, let alone devise, such a model of statecraft. It came from an Irregular Circle who, instead of being destroyed in childhood, was spared by foolish indulgence to bring disaster to his country and destruction to millions of followers.

On one hand, the proposition was designed to bring Women of all classes over to the side of the Chromatic Innovation. By giving Women the same two colors as the Priests, the Revolutionists ensured that in certain positions, every Woman would appear as a Priest and be treated with corresponding respect and deference - a prospect that couldn't fail to attract the Female Sex en masse.

But some of my Readers might not recognize how Priests and Women could look identical under this new legislation. If so, a word or two will make it clear.

Imagine a woman decorated according to the new Code: her front half (the half with the eye and mouth) is red, and her back half is green. Look at her from the side. Obviously, you'll see a straight line, half red and half green.

Now imagine a Priest, whose mouth is at M, and whose front semicircle (AMB) is therefore colored red, while his back semicircle is green, so that the diameter AB divides the green from the red. If you look at the Great Man so that your eye is in the same straight line as his dividing diameter (AB), what you'll see will be a straight line (CBD), of which one half (CB) will be red, and the other (BD) green. The whole line (CD) will be a bit shorter than that of a full-sized Woman, and will fade more quickly towards its ends, but the similarity of the colors would give you an immediate impression of similarity in Class, making you overlook other details.

Remember the decay of Sight Recognition that was threatening society at the time of the Color revolt. Add to that the certainty that Women would quickly learn to shade their extremities to imitate the Circles. It must then be obvious to you, my dear Reader, that the Color Bill put us in great danger of confusing a Priest with a young Woman.

How attractive this prospect must have been to the Frail Sex! They looked forward with delight to the confusion that would follow. At home, they might overhear political and ecclesiastical secrets meant for their husbands and brothers, and might even issue commands in the name of a priestly Circle. Outside, the striking combination of red and green without any other colors would surely lead common people into endless mistakes, and Women would gain whatever respect the Circles lost from passersby. As for the scandal that would befall the Circular Class if the frivolous and unseemly conduct of the Women were blamed on them, and the consequent subversion of the Constitution, the Female Sex couldn't be expected to give a thought to these considerations. Even in the households of the Circles, the Women were all in favor of the Universal Color Bill.

The second aim of the Bill was the gradual demoralization of the Circles themselves. In the general intellectual decay, they still maintained their pristine clearness and strength of understanding. From their earliest childhood, growing up in Circular households completely devoid of Color, the Nobles alone preserved the Sacred Art of Sight Recognition, with all the advantages that come from that excellent training of the intellect. Thus, up until the introduction of the Universal Color Bill, the Circles had not only held their own but even increased their lead over the other classes by abstaining from the popular fashion.

Now, therefore, the artful Irregular whom I described above as the real author of this diabolical Bill determined to bring down the status of the Hierarchy in one fell swoop. He would force them to submit to the pollution of Color, and at the same time destroy their domestic opportunities to train in the Art of Sight Recognition, thus weakening their intellects by depriving them of their pure and colorless homes. Once subjected to the chromatic taint, every parental and childish Circle would corrupt each other. Only in distinguishing between Father and Mother would the Circular infant find problems to exercise its understanding - problems too often likely to be corrupted by maternal deceptions, shaking the child's faith in all logical conclusions. Thus, bit by bit, the intellectual brilliance of the Priestly Order would fade, paving the way for a total destruction of all Aristocratic Legislature and for the subversion of our Privileged Classes.

Chapter 10: How the Color Rebellion Was Crushed

The campaign for the Universal Color Bill went on for three years. Right up to the last moment, it looked like chaos was going to win.

A whole army of Polygons, who had signed up to fight as regular soldiers, was completely wiped out by a larger force of Isosceles Triangles. Meanwhile, the Squares and Pentagons stayed neutral.

Even worse, some of the most capable Circles fell victim to domestic violence. Driven mad by political anger, the wives in many noble households pestered their husbands endlessly to give up their opposition to the Color Bill. Some, finding their pleas ignored, went berserk and killed their innocent children and husband, then took their own lives in the act of violence. It's recorded that during this three-year period, no fewer than twenty-three Circles died in domestic disputes.

The danger was extreme. It seemed the Priests had no choice but to either give in or be wiped out. But then, the course of events was completely changed by one of those dramatic incidents that politicians should never ignore, often anticipate, and sometimes even create. These incidents have an absurdly disproportionate power to sway the public's emotions.

Here's what happened: An Isosceles of a low type, with a brain barely above four degrees, was messing around with some paints he'd stolen from a shopkeeper. He either painted himself or got someone else to paint him (the story varies) with the twelve colors of a Dodecagon. He went into the Market Place and, disguising his voice, approached an orphan girl who was the daughter of a noble Polygon. In the past, he had tried unsuccessfully to win her affection. Through a series of deceptions – helped on one hand by a string of lucky accidents too long to relate, and on the other by an almost unbelievable carelessness on the part of the girl's relatives – he managed to marry her. The unhappy girl committed suicide when she discovered the fraud she had been subjected to.

When news of this tragedy spread from State to State, it deeply affected the minds of the Women. They sympathized with the miserable victim and worried that they, their sisters, or their daughters might face similar deceptions. This made them see the Color Bill in a completely new light. Many openly admitted they had changed their minds; the rest just needed a little push to make a similar admission. Seizing this favorable opportunity, the Circles quickly called an extraordinary Assembly of the States. Besides the usual guard of Convicts, they made sure a large number of reactionary Women attended.

Amid an unprecedented crowd, the Chief Circle of those days – Pantocyclus by name – stood up to find himself hissed and booed by a hundred and twenty thousand Isosceles. But he quieted them by announcing that from now on, the Circles would adopt a policy of Concession; they would accept the Color Bill to satisfy the majority's wishes. The jeers immediately turned to applause, and he invited Chromatistes, the leader of the Sedition, to come to the center of the hall to receive the submission of the Hierarchy on behalf of his followers. Then followed a speech, a masterpiece of rhetoric, which took nearly a day to deliver, and which no summary can do justice to.

With a show of impartiality, he declared that as they were now finally committing to Reform or Innovation, they should take one last look at the pros and cons of the whole subject, its flaws as well as its advantages. Gradually bringing up the dangers to the Tradesmen, the Professional Classes and the Gentlemen, he silenced the rising murmurs of the Isosceles by reminding them that, despite all these flaws, he was willing to accept the Bill if the majority approved it. But it was clear that everyone except the Isosceles was moved by his words and was either neutral or against the Bill.

Turning to the Workmen, he insisted that their interests must not be neglected, and that if they intended to accept the Color Bill, they should at least do so with a full understanding of the consequences. Many of them, he said, were on the verge of being admitted to the class of the Regular Triangles; others hoped their children would achieve a distinction they couldn't hope for themselves. That honorable ambition would have to be sacrificed. With the universal adoption of Color, all distinctions would disappear; Regularity would be confused with Irregularity; development would give way to regression; the Workman would, in a few generations, be degraded to the level of the Military, or even the Convict Class; political power would be in the hands of the greatest number, that is to say the Criminal Classes, who were already more numerous than the Workmen, and would soon outnumber all the other Classes put together when the usual Compensative Laws of Nature were violated.

A subdued murmur of assent ran through the ranks of the Artisans, and Chromatistes, in alarm, tried to step forward and address them. But he found himself surrounded by guards and forced to remain silent while the Chief Circle made a final passionate appeal to the Women. He exclaimed that if the Color Bill passed, no marriage would be safe, no woman's honor secure; fraud, deception, and hypocrisy would invade every household; domestic happiness would share the fate of the Constitution and quickly perish. "Rather than this," he cried, "come death!"

At these words, which were the prearranged signal for action, the Isosceles Convicts fell on and stabbed the wretched Chromatistes; the Regular Classes, opening their ranks, made way for a band of Women who, under the Circles' direction, moved backwards, invisibly and unerringly upon the unconscious soldiers; the Artisans, imitating their betters, also opened their ranks. Meanwhile, bands of Convicts occupied every entrance with an impenetrable phalanx.

The battle, or rather carnage, was short-lived. Under the skillful generalship of the Circles, almost every Woman's charge was fatal and many extracted their sting uninjured, ready for a second slaughter. But no second blow was needed; the rabble of the Isosceles did the rest of the job themselves. Surprised, leaderless, attacked in front by invisible foes, and finding escape cut off by the Convicts behind them, they lost all presence of mind, as is their habit, and raised the cry of "treachery." This sealed their fate. Every Isosceles now saw an enemy in every other. In half an hour, not one of that vast multitude was left alive; and the fragments of seven score thousand of the Criminal Class slain by one another's angles attested to the triumph of Order.

The Circles didn't delay in pushing their victory to the utmost. The Working Men they spared but decimated. The Militia of the Equilaterals was immediately called out, and every Triangle suspected of Irregularity on reasonable grounds was destroyed by Court Martial, without the formality of exact measurement by the Social Board. The homes of the Military and Artisan classes were inspected in a course of visitations extending through upwards of a year; and during that period, every town, village, and hamlet was systematically purged of that excess of the lower orders which had been brought about by the neglect to pay the tribute of Criminals to the Schools and University, and by the violation of other natural Laws of the Constitution of Flatland. Thus the balance of classes was again restored.

Needless to say, from then on, the use of Color was abolished, and its possession prohibited. Even the utterance of any word denoting Color, except by the Circles or by qualified scientific teachers, was punished by a severe penalty. Only at our University in some of the very highest and most esoteric classes—which I myself have never been privileged to attend—it is understood that the sparing use of Color is still sanctioned for the purpose of illustrating some of the deeper problems of mathematics. But of this I can only speak from hearsay.

Elsewhere in Flatland, Color is now non-existent. The art of making it is known to only one living person, the Chief Circle for the time being; and by him it is handed down on his death-bed to none but his Successor. One factory alone produces it; and, to prevent the secret from being betrayed, the Workmen are annually consumed, and fresh ones introduced. So great is the terror with which even now our Aristocracy looks back to the far-distant days of the agitation for the Universal Color Bill.

Chapter 11: About Our Priests

It's high time I move on from these brief and scattered notes about life in Flatland to the main event of this book: my introduction to the mysteries of Space. That's my real subject; everything that's come before is just a preface.

For this reason, I have to skip over many topics that I think would interest my readers. For example, I won't explain how we move around and stop ourselves, even though we don't have feet; or how we make buildings stable out of wood, stone, or brick, even though we don't have hands and can't lay foundations like you can, or use the sideways pressure of the earth; or how rain forms in the gaps between our different zones, so that the northern regions don't block the moisture falling on the southern parts; or about the nature of our hills and mines, our trees and vegetables, our seasons and harvests; our alphabet and way of writing, adapted to our linear tablets. These and a hundred other details of our physical existence I have to pass over. I'm not mentioning them now just to let my readers know that I haven't forgotten about them, but because I'm thinking about the reader's time.

But before I get to my main subject, my readers will probably expect a few final remarks about these pillars and mainstays of our Constitution, the controllers of our behavior and shapers of our destiny, the objects of universal respect and almost worship: I mean, of course, our Circles or Priests.

When I call them Priests, don't think I mean just what that term means to you. With us, our Priests are in charge of all Business, Art, and Science; they're Directors of Trade, Commerce, Generalship, Architecture, Engineering, Education, Statesmanship, Legislature, Morality, Theology; they don't do anything themselves, but they're the reason for everything worth doing that's done by others.

Although everyone popularly called a Circle is thought to be a Circle, among the better educated Classes it's known that no Circle is really a Circle, but only a Polygon with a very large number of very small sides. As the number of sides increases, a Polygon gets closer to being a Circle; and when the number is very large indeed, say three or four hundred, it's extremely difficult for even the most delicate touch to feel any polygonal angles. Let me say rather, it would be difficult: because, as I've explained before, Recognition by Feeling is unknown among the highest society, and to feel a Circle would be considered a very rude insult. This habit of not Feeling in the best society makes it easier for a Circle to keep up the mystery about the exact nature of his Perimeter or Circumference, which he's been used to wrapping in secrecy from his earliest years. Three feet being the average Perimeter, it follows that in a Polygon of three hundred sides, each side will be no more than the hundredth part of a foot in length, or little more than the tenth part of an inch; and in a Polygon of six or seven hundred sides, the sides are little larger than the diameter of a Spaceland pin-head. It's always assumed, out of politeness, that the Chief Circle for the time being has ten thousand sides.

The rise of the posterity of the Circles in the social scale isn't restricted, as it is among the lower Regular classes, by the Law of Nature which limits the increase of sides to one in each generation. Chapter 13: My Dream of Lineland

It was December 30, 1999, the second-to-last day of our year. I had been enjoying my favorite pastime, Geometry, late into the night and went to bed with an unsolved problem on my mind. That night, I had a strange dream.

I saw before me a vast crowd of small Straight Lines. I assumed these were Women, mixed with other, even smaller beings that looked like bright dots. All of these were moving back and forth along the same Straight Line, seemingly at the same speed.

They made a confused, chirping noise while they moved. Sometimes they'd stop moving, and then everything would go quiet.

I approached one of the larger Lines, which I thought was a Woman, and tried to speak to her. I got no answer. I tried again, and again, but still nothing. Getting frustrated at what seemed like incredibly rude behavior, I positioned myself directly in front of her to block her path. Then I loudly repeated my question: "Woman, what's going on here? What's all this chirping about, and why is everyone moving back and forth on the same Straight Line?"

"I'm not a Woman," the small Line replied. "I am the Ruler of this world. But you, where do you come from, intruding into my kingdom of Lineland?"

Surprised by this abrupt response, I apologized if I had startled or bothered His Royal Highness. I explained that I was a stranger and asked if he could tell me about his kingdom. But I had a really hard time getting any useful information. The King kept assuming that whatever he knew, I must know too, and that I was just pretending to be ignorant as a joke. But by asking a lot of questions, I managed to learn the following:

It turned out that this poor, clueless King believed that the Straight Line he called his Kingdom – the Line he spent his whole existence on – was the entire world, indeed the whole of Space itself. Since he couldn't move or see except along his Straight Line, he couldn't even imagine anything outside of it.

When I first spoke to him, he had heard my voice, but the sound had come to him in a way that was completely foreign to his experience. As he put it, he "heard a voice as if from my own insides." It wasn't until I put my mouth in his world that he had any sense of me at all. Even then, he had no idea about the region I came from. Outside his World, or Line, everything was a blank to him. No, not even a blank, because a blank implies Space. To him, everything outside his Line simply didn't exist.

All of his subjects – the small Lines he called men and the Points he called women – were also confined in their movement and sight to that single Straight Line, which was their entire World. Their whole horizon was limited to a Point; no one could ever see anything but a Point.

In Lineland, you could only tell the difference between a man, woman, or child by the sound of their voice. Also, because each individual occupied the whole of the narrow path that made up their Universe, no one could ever pass another. Once you were neighbors with someone, you were always neighbors. Being neighbors in Lineland was like being married in our world – you stayed that way until death parted you.

To me, such a life seemed incredibly boring – all vision limited to a Point, all movement confined to a Straight Line. I was surprised to see how lively and cheerful the King seemed. Wondering if it was possible to have any kind of family life under such limiting circumstances, I hesitated for a while before asking about such a delicate subject. But finally, I took the plunge and asked about the health of his family.

"My wives and children are well and happy," he replied.

I was shocked by this answer. In the immediate vicinity of the King, I had noticed there were only Men. So I ventured to reply, "Pardon me, but I can't imagine how Your Royal Highness can ever see or approach your Family. There are at least half a dozen individuals between you and them, whom you can neither see through nor pass by. Is it possible that in Lineland, closeness isn't necessary for marriage or having children?"

"What an absurd question!" replied the Monarch. "If it were as you suggest, the Universe would soon be empty. No, no; nearness isn't necessary for love, and the birth of children is far too important to depend on such an accident as proximity. You can't be ignorant of this. But since you're pretending not to know, I'll explain it to you as if you were a baby in Lineland.

"Know, then, that marriages are consummated by the sense of sound and the ability to hear. You're aware, of course, that every Man has two mouths or voices – as well as two eyes – a bass at one end and a tenor at the other. I wouldn't mention this, but I haven't been able to hear your tenor during our conversation."

I replied that I only had one voice, and I hadn't known that His Royal Highness had two. "That confirms my impression," said the King, "that you're not a Man, but some kind of monstrous being with a bass voice and a completely uneducated ear. But let me continue.

"Nature itself has decreed that every Man should marry two wives—" "Why two?" I asked. "You're taking your pretend simplicity too far," he cried. "How can there be a completely harmonious union without the combination of Four in One, namely, the Bass and Tenor of the Man and the Soprano and Contralto of the two Women?" "But suppose," I said, "that a man should prefer one wife or three?" "That's impossible," he said. "It's as unthinkable as the idea that two and one could make five, or that the human eye could see a Straight Line."

In the middle of this group dance, at the fifty-first beat, all the inhabitants of the Universe pause in their movement, and each individual sends out their richest, fullest, sweetest sound. "Bass-voiced Monster! Yes," replied the King. "How else could we maintain the balance between the sexes if two girls weren't born for every boy? Would you ignore the very basics of Nature?" Chapter 14: My Failed Attempt to Explain Flatland

I thought it was time to bring the King down from his high horse and talk some sense into him. I decided to try and open his eyes to the truth - or at least, to help him understand what things are really like in Flatland. So I began:

"Your Majesty, how do you tell the difference between your subjects' shapes and positions? For my part, before I entered your kingdom, I noticed by sight that some of your people are lines and others are points. I also saw that some lines are larger -"

The King cut me off. "You're talking nonsense!" he interrupted. "You must have been hallucinating. Everyone knows it's impossible to see the difference between a Line and a Point just by looking. You can only tell the difference by hearing. And that's also how you can figure out my shape exactly. Look at me - I'm a Line, the longest in Lineland, over six inches of Space -"

"You mean Length," I suggested.

"Fool," he snapped. "Space is Length. Interrupt me again, and I'm done talking."

I apologized, but he continued scornfully, "Since you're too thick to understand logic, I'll let you hear for yourself how I use my two voices to show my shape to my Wives. They're currently six thousand miles, seventy yards, two feet, and eight inches away - one to the North, the other to the South. Listen, I'm calling to them."

He made a chirping sound, then went on smugly: "Right now, my wives are hearing one of my voices, quickly followed by the other. They can tell that the second voice reaches them after an interval in which sound can travel 6.457 inches. Of course, my wives don't do this calculation every time they hear my two voices. They did it once, before we got married. But they could do it anytime if they wanted to. This was, of course, a pretty stupid question. Touch wouldn't have solved the problem at all. But I asked it to annoy the King, and it worked perfectly.

"What?!" he cried in horror. "Explain what you mean."

"Feel, touch, come into contact," I replied.

"If by 'feeling' you mean getting so close that there's no space between two individuals," said the King, "you should know that in my kingdom, this crime is punishable by death. "Besides, what possible use could there be for this illegal and unnatural 'touching' you're talking about? All the benefits of such a crude process are achieved more easily and accurately just by hearing. As for your suggestion about the risk of deception, it doesn't exist. A person's voice is the essence of their being - it can't be changed at will. But come on, let's say I could pass through solid objects and penetrate my subjects one by one, even up to a billion of them, checking the size and distance of each by 'feeling'. How much time and energy would be wasted on this clumsy and inaccurate method! Whereas now, in a single moment of listening, I can take a census and get statistics - local, physical, mental, and spiritual - of every living being in Lineland. Just listen!"

To see nothing but a Point! Not even being able to imagine a Straight Line! In fact, not even knowing what a Straight Line is! To see, yet be cut off from the linear views we have in Flatland! Surely it's better to have no sight at all than to see so little! I admit I don't have your impressive hearing ability. The concert of all Lineland that gives you such joy sounds like nothing more than a lot of twittering or chirping to me. But at least I can see the difference between a Line and a Point with my eyes. King: Show me, if you please, this motion from left to right.

Me: I'm afraid I can't do that unless you could step out of your Line altogether.

King: Out of my Line? Do you mean out of the world? Out of Space?

Me: Well, yes. Out of your world. Out of your Space. Because your Space isn't the true Space. True Space is a Plane, but your Space is only a Line.

King: If you can't demonstrate this motion from left to right by moving yourself, then please describe it to me in words.

Me: If you can't tell your right side from your left, I'm afraid no words of mine can make my meaning any clearer to you. But surely you can't be ignorant of such a simple distinction.

Hearing these words, the King rushed towards me with a threatening cry as if to stab me through my diagonal. Chapter 15: A Visitor from the 3D World

It was New Year's Eve, 1999 - the last day of our millennium. The sound of rain outside told me it was night. I was sitting with my wife, thinking about the past year and what the future might bring - not just for the next year, but for the new century and even the new millennium.

My four sons and two orphaned grandchildren had gone to bed. Only my wife was still with me, waiting to see the old millennium out and the new one in.

I was lost in thought, mulling over something my youngest grandson, a bright young hexagon, had said earlier. My uncles and I had been giving him his usual lesson in recognizing shapes by sight. I took nine squares, each one inch on all sides, and arranged them to make one big square that was three inches on each side. "Nothing at all," I replied, "at least not in geometry as we know it. Our geometry only has two dimensions."

"What's wrong?" my wife asked. "There's no draft. What are you looking for? There's nothing there."

Both my wife and I heard the words, though she didn't understand what they meant. We both leaped up, looking in the direction of the sound. To our horror, we saw a Figure!

At first glance, it looked like a woman seen from the side. But after a moment, I realized the ends faded too quickly for it to be female. I thought it might be a circle, except it seemed to change size in a way that was impossible for any regular shape I knew of.

My wife, not having my experience or the cool head needed to notice these details, jumped to the conclusion that a woman had somehow gotten into the house. "How did this person get in?" she exclaimed. "You promised me there wouldn't be any vents in our new house."

Chapter 16: How the Stranger Tried and Failed to Explain Spaceland to Me Using Just Words

"I knew it!" I exclaimed. "I was sure my guess would be right. And now, please be patient with me and answer one more question, my excellent Teacher! Those who have appeared - no one knows where they came from - and then vanished - no one knows where to - have they also shrunk and disappeared somehow into that more spacious Space, where I'm now begging you to take me?"

The Sphere replied in a gloomy tone, "They've vanished, for sure - if they ever really appeared. But most people think these visions came from the mind - you won't understand this - from the brain. They say it's because of the disturbed perceptions of the person who saw them."

"Say it isn't so!" I cried. "Don't believe them. Or if it really is true that this other Space is actually the land of Thought, then take me to that blessed region where I can see inside all solid things. There, before my amazed eyes, a Cube will move in a completely new direction, but still following the rules, so that every part of its insides will pass through a new kind of Space. It'll create an even more perfect shape than itself, with sixteen corners and Eight solid Cubes as its outline. And once we're there, shall we stop our upward journey? In that wonderful region of Four Dimensions, won't we look to the threshold of the Fifth, and not enter? Ah, no! Let's keep going, pushing our mental boundaries higher. Then, giving in to our intellectual drive, the gates of the Sixth Dimension will open wide. After that, a Seventh, and then an Eighth—"

I don't know how long I would have continued if the Sphere hadn't interrupted me, despite his thunderous commands for silence and threats of terrible punishments if I kept talking. Nothing could stop the flood of my excited aspirations. Maybe I was to blame, but honestly, I was drunk on the recent doses of Truth he had given me. I couldn't help myself. However, the end came quickly. My words were cut short by a crash outside, and a simultaneous crash inside me, which sent me flying through space with a speed that made speech impossible. Down! Down! I was rapidly descending, and I knew that return to Flatland was my fate. One glimpse, one last and never-to-be-forgotten glimpse I had of that dull level wilderness - which was now to become my Universe again - spread out before my eye. Then a darkness. Then a final, all-encompassing thunder-peal; and when I came to myself, I was once again a common creeping Square, in my study at home, listening to the Peace-Cry of my approaching Wife.

Chapter 20: How the Sphere Encouraged Me in a Dream

Even though I had less than a minute to think, I instinctively knew I had to hide what happened from my wife. It wasn't that I was worried she'd tell anyone my secret. I just knew that, as a woman in Flatland, she wouldn't be able to understand my adventures.

So I made up a story on the spot. I told her I had accidentally fallen through the cellar's trapdoor and been knocked out.

In our country, the pull towards the South is so weak that even for a woman, my story seemed pretty unbelievable. But my wife, who's much smarter than most women, could tell I was really worked up about something. She didn't argue with me. Instead, she insisted I was sick and needed to rest.

I was glad for an excuse to go to my room and think quietly about what had happened. When I was finally alone, I started feeling sleepy. But before I closed my eyes, I tried to picture the Third Dimension in my mind, especially how a cube is made by moving a square. It wasn't as clear as I wanted it to be. But I remembered that the key was to think "Upward, but not Northward." I was determined to keep these words in my mind as a clue that would lead me to the answer. So, like a magic spell, I kept repeating "Upward, but not Northward" until I fell into a deep sleep.

While I was sleeping, I had a dream. I thought I was once again with the Sphere, who was now glowing brightly, showing he wasn't angry with me anymore. We were moving together towards a tiny, bright dot. The Sphere pointed it out to me. As we got closer, I thought I heard a faint humming noise coming from it, like a very quiet version of the buzzing you might hear from a fly in your world. The sound was so soft that even in the total silence of the empty space we were floating through, we couldn't hear it until we stopped moving when we were about twenty human diagonals away from it.

"Look over there," said my guide. "You've lived in Flatland, you've seen Lineland, and you've soared with me to the heights of Spaceland. Now, to complete your experience, I'm taking you down to the lowest level of existence - to Pointland, the Abyss of No Dimensions."

"See that miserable creature? That Point is a being like us, but it's trapped in a world with no dimensions at all. It is its own world, its own universe. It can't even imagine anything other than itself. It doesn't know about length, width, or height because it's never experienced them. It can't even understand the concept of 'two' - it has no idea what 'more than one' means. To this Point, it is everything that exists, even though it's really nothing at all."

"Look at how perfectly happy it is with itself," the Sphere continued. "Learn this lesson from it: being content with yourself when you know nothing is a terrible thing. It's better to want to learn and grow, even if it's difficult, than to be blindly and stupidly happy."

"Now, listen," he said.

The Sphere stopped talking, and I heard a tiny, low, monotonous sound coming from the little buzzing creature. It sounded like one of your world's voice recorders, and I could make out these words: "Infinite happiness of existence! It is; and there is nothing else besides It."

"What does the tiny creature mean by 'it'?" I asked.

"It means itself," said the Sphere. "Haven't you noticed before how babies and simple-minded people who can't tell the difference between themselves and the world around them often talk about themselves as if they were someone else?"

"But be quiet and listen," he added.

The little creature kept talking to itself: "It fills all of Space, and what It fills, It is. What It thinks, It says out loud. What It says, It hears. It is the Thinker, the Speaker, the Hearer, the Thought, the Word, and the Sound. It is the One, and yet It is All in All. Oh, the happiness, the happiness of Being!"

"Can't you shock the little thing out of its self-satisfaction?" I asked. "Tell it what it really is, like you told me. Show it how limited Pointland is, and inspire it to aim for something higher."

"That's not an easy task," said my teacher. "Why don't you try?"

So, raising my voice as loud as I could, I addressed the Point:

"Silence, you pitiful creature. You call yourself the All in All, but you are Nothing. Your so-called Universe is just a tiny speck in a Line, and a Line is just a shadow compared to—"

"Shh, that's enough," the Sphere interrupted. "Now, watch how the King of Pointland reacts to your speech."

The Point glowed even brighter when it heard my words, showing it was still perfectly happy with itself. I had barely finished speaking when it started talking again.

"Ah, the joy, the joy of Thought! What can It not achieve by thinking! Its own Thought coming to Itself, suggesting Its imperfection, only to enhance Its happiness! Sweet rebellion stirred up to end in triumph! Ah, the divine creative power of the All in One! Ah, the joy, the joy of Being!"

"You see," said my teacher, "how little effect your words have had. As far as the monarch understands them at all, it thinks they are its own thoughts. It can't imagine any other being besides itself. It's proud of the variety of 'Its Thought' and sees it as proof of its creative power. Let's leave this God of Pointland to enjoy its ignorant self-satisfaction. Nothing you or I can do will free it from its self-obsession."

After this, as we gently floated back to Flatland, I could hear my companion's calm voice explaining the moral of my vision and encouraging me to aspire to greater things and to teach others to do the same. He admitted that at first, he had been angry at my ambition to understand dimensions beyond the Third. But since then, he had gained new insight and wasn't too proud to admit his mistake to a student.

Then he began to teach me even higher mysteries than those I had seen before. He showed me how to create Extra-Solids by moving Solids, and Double Extra-Solids by moving Extra-Solids. All of this was "strictly according to analogy," using methods so simple and easy that even women could understand them.

Chapter 21: How I Tried to Teach the Theory of Three Dimensions to My Grandson, and What Happened

I woke up feeling excited and started thinking about the amazing opportunities ahead of me. I was determined to spread the word about the Third Dimension throughout Flatland. I decided I would start by telling my wife.

Just as I was planning what to do, I heard people in the street calling for silence. Then a louder voice spoke - it was an official announcement. I listened carefully and realized it was the Council's decision. They were ordering the arrest, imprisonment, or execution of anyone who tried to mislead people with false ideas or claimed to have received messages from another world.

I thought about this for a while. It was clearly too dangerous to ignore. I decided it would be safer to avoid mentioning my experience in the Third Dimension. Instead, I would try to prove it through logic and reasoning. After all, the idea seemed so simple and clear - "Upward, not Northward" was the key to understanding it all. It had seemed obvious to me before I fell asleep, and when I first woke up, it was as clear as basic math. But now, it didn't seem quite so simple.

Even though my wife came into the room at that moment, I decided not to start explaining it to her after we exchanged a few casual words.

My sons, who were respected doctors, weren't great at math and wouldn't be suitable for what I had in mind. But then I thought of my young, intelligent grandson who was good with numbers. I remembered how he had impressed me earlier with his thoughts about what 3³ might mean. I figured it would be safe to start with him since he was just a child and wouldn't know about the Council's announcement. If I talked about controversial topics with my sons, I couldn't be sure they wouldn't report me to the authorities out of a sense of duty, despite their love for me.

But first, I had to come up with a story to explain to my wife why the Circle had wanted to meet with me and how he had entered our house. I won't go into the details of the elaborate tale I told her, but let's just say it wasn't entirely truthful. In the end, I managed to convince her to go back to her household duties without mentioning anything about the World of Three Dimensions.

With that taken care of, I immediately sent for my grandson. To be honest, everything I had seen and heard was starting to feel like a fading dream, and I was eager to try teaching someone else about it.

When my grandson entered the room, I carefully closed the door. Then, sitting next to him with our math tools, I said we'd continue yesterday's lesson. I showed him again how a Point moving in One Dimension creates a Line, and how a Line moving in Two Dimensions creates a Square. Then, trying to sound casual, I said, "Now, you little rascal, you were trying to convince me that a Square could somehow move 'Upward, not Northward' and create another shape, a kind of extra square in Three Dimensions. Let's hear that idea again."

Just then, we heard the official announcement being repeated outside in the street. Even though he was young, my grandson - who was unusually smart for his age and had been taught to always respect the Circles - understood what was happening faster than I expected. He waited until the announcement finished, then burst into tears and said, "Grandpa, I was only joking earlier. Of course I didn't mean anything by it. We didn't know about the new law then, and I don't think I said anything about a Third Dimension. I'm sure I never said 'Upward, not Northward' because that doesn't make any sense. How could something move Upward and not Northward? That's just silly. Even a baby wouldn't say something so ridiculous. It's so funny! Ha ha ha!"

"It's not silly at all," I said, starting to lose my temper. "Look, I'll show you. Here's a Square," I said, picking up a movable Square that was nearby. "Now I'm moving it, not Northward but... yes, I'm moving it Upward. Well, not exactly like this, but somehow..." I trailed off, waving the Square around aimlessly, which only made my grandson laugh even harder.

He said I wasn't teaching him anything, just playing around. Then he unlocked the door and ran out of the room. And that's how my first attempt to teach someone about the Third Dimension ended in failure.

Chapter 22: How I Tried to Spread the Theory of Three Dimensions Using Different Methods, and What Happened

My failure with my grandson didn't discourage me from sharing my secret with others in my household. But it did teach me that I couldn't rely solely on the phrase "Upward, not Northward." I realized I needed to give people a clearer picture of the whole concept. To do this, I decided I had to write it down.

So, I spent several months in private, writing a book about the mysteries of Three Dimensions. To try to avoid breaking the law, I didn't talk about a physical Dimension. Instead, I described a "Thoughtland" - a place where, in theory, a shape could look down on Flatland and see inside everything at once. In this place, there might even be a shape surrounded by six squares and with eight corner points.

But as I was writing, I ran into a big problem. In our country of Flatland, we don't have any writing surfaces except Lines. We don't have any diagrams except Lines, all in one straight line, only distinguishable by how big or bright they are. So when I finished my book (which I called "Through Flatland to Thoughtland"), I wasn't sure if many people would understand what I meant.

Meanwhile, my wife was feeling down. I lost interest in all the things I used to enjoy. Everything I saw tempted me to speak out about what I knew, because I couldn't compare what I saw in Two Dimensions with what it really was in Three Dimensions. It was hard not to make these comparisons out loud.

I neglected my clients and my own work, spending all my time thinking about the mysteries I had seen but couldn't share with anyone. Every day, it got harder to remember exactly what I had experienced. One day, about eleven months after I returned from Spaceland, I tried to picture a Cube with my eyes closed, but I couldn't do it. Even though I managed to do it later, I was never quite sure if I was remembering it correctly. This made me even more depressed than before, and I decided I had to do something, but I didn't know what.

I felt like I would have given my life for the cause if it meant I could convince people. But if I couldn't even convince my own grandson, how could I hope to convince the highest and most developed Circles in the land?

Sometimes my feelings were too strong to keep inside, and I would say things that could get me in trouble. People already thought I had strange ideas, maybe even dangerous ones. I knew I was in a risky situation, but sometimes I couldn't stop myself from saying suspicious things, even when I was with high-ranking Polygons or Circles.

For example, when people were discussing how to deal with those who claimed they could see inside things, I would quote an old Circle who said that most people always think prophets and visionaries are crazy. I sometimes used phrases like "the eye that sees inside things" and "the all-seeing land." Once or twice, I even used the forbidden terms "Third and Fourth Dimensions."

Finally, I made a big mistake. At a meeting of our local discussion group, held at the Prefect's own house, someone read a long paper explaining why Providence had limited the number of Dimensions to Two, and why only the Supreme Being could see everything. I forgot myself and gave a detailed account of my whole journey with the Sphere into Space, to the Assembly Hall in our capital, then back to Space, and finally my return home. I told them everything I had seen and heard, both real and in visions.

At first, I pretended I was describing the imaginary experiences of a made-up person. But I got so excited that I soon dropped the act. In the end, I passionately urged everyone listening to forget their preconceptions and believe in the Third Dimension.

You can probably guess what happened next - I was immediately arrested and taken before the Council.

The next morning, I found myself standing in the very spot where the Sphere had stood with me just a few months earlier. I was allowed to tell my story without interruption. But from the start, I knew what was going to happen. The President noticed that there were high-ranking Policemen on guard, with angles of nearly 55°. He ordered them to be replaced by lower-ranking ones of 2° or 3°. I knew all too well what this meant.

I was going to be executed or imprisoned, and my story was going to be kept secret by getting rid of the officials who had heard it. Since this was the plan, the President wanted to use cheaper victims instead of more expensive ones.

After I finished explaining myself, the President - maybe noticing that some of the younger Circles seemed moved by how sincere I was - asked me two questions:

1. Could I show the direction I meant when I said "Upward, not Northward"?

2. Could I use any diagrams or descriptions (other than just listing imaginary sides and angles) to show the shape I was calling a Cube?

I said I couldn't say anything more, and that I had to trust in the Truth, which would surely win in the end.

The President agreed with my sentiment and said I couldn't do better. He said I would be sentenced to life in prison. But if the Truth wanted me to get out of prison and spread the message to the world, then the Truth could make that happen. In the meantime, I wouldn't be made uncomfortable except for what was necessary to keep me from escaping. Unless I misbehaved, I would occasionally be allowed to see my brother, who was already in prison.

Seven years have passed, and I'm still a prisoner. Except for occasional visits from my brother, I'm not allowed to have any companions other than my jailers. My brother is one of the best Squares - fair, sensible, cheerful, and caring about his family. But I have to admit that our weekly meetings cause me a lot of pain in one way.

He was there when the Sphere appeared in the Council Chamber. He saw the Sphere's changing cross-sections. He heard the explanation given to the Circles about what was happening. For the past seven years, almost every week, he has heard me repeat the story of my part in that event. I've described all the phenomena in Spaceland and explained the arguments for the existence of Solid things based on Analogy. Yet - I'm ashamed to admit - my brother still hasn't understood the nature of Three Dimensions. He openly says he doesn't believe a Sphere exists.

So, I have no converts at all. As far as I can tell, this world-changing revelation has been given to me for nothing. In the myth, Prometheus was punished for bringing fire to humans. But I - a Flatland Prometheus - am locked up here for bringing nothing to my countrymen.

Still, I hold onto the hope that these memoirs will somehow find their way to the minds of humanity in Some Dimension, and might inspire a group of rebels who refuse to be limited to a set number of Dimensions.

That's what I hope for in my brighter moments. But it's not always like that. Sometimes, I'm weighed down by the troubling thought that I can't even be sure of the exact shape of the Cube I once saw and now miss so much. In my dreams at night, the mysterious instruction "Upward, not Northward" haunts me like a riddle I can't solve.

It's part of the suffering I endure for the sake of Truth that there are times when my mind is weak. At these times, Cubes and Spheres seem to fade away into things that might not even exist. The Land of Three Dimensions starts to feel almost as unreal as the Land of One or No Dimensions. Even this solid wall that keeps me from freedom, these very tablets I'm writing on, and all the solid realities of Flatland itself, seem like nothing more than products of a troubled imagination or the flimsy fabric of a dream.
